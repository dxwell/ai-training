About This Training

A 90-minute hands-on session that teaches mixed actuarial and risk/compliance teams how to validate AI models and apply governance frameworks — grounded in Australian regulatory context and real insurance scenarios. Every concept is shown before it is explained.

Learning Outcomes
01
Identify the three failure modes of AI models in actuarial work — data drift, proxy discrimination, and opacity — and recognise them in a live example.
02
Apply APRA CPG 234 and the Actuaries Institute AI guidance to a model validation checklist for a pricing or reserving use case.
03
Distinguish model risk levels using a simple tiering framework and assign appropriate validation requirements to each tier.
04
Write a model risk finding in plain language that satisfies both the Appointed Actuary and the CRO — without actuarial jargon on one hand or vague risk-speak on the other.
05
Understand where each discipline fits — actuaries, risk, and compliance — in the three lines of defence for AI governance.
Design Principles
Show First, Explain Second
Every concept begins with a live demonstration or a concrete case before the framework is named. Participants see the problem before they see the solution.
Australian Insurance Context
All examples use Australian data, APRA/ASIC regulatory anchors, and scenarios from general, life, or health insurance — not generic ML examples.
Two Disciplines, One Room
Activities are structured so actuaries and risk/compliance professionals each bring distinct expertise. Neither group can complete the task alone.

0:00
Setup
The Burning Platform (5 min)
Facilitator opens with a real-world AI model failure in Australian insurance — no slides, just a story. Participants are asked: "Who was responsible? How would you have caught it?"
Anchor case: A motor insurer's gradient boosting pricing model silently degraded after COVID lockdowns changed driving patterns. Premiums diverged from actual risk by 18% before discovery. The model passed its original validation — but nobody owned ongoing monitoring.
0:05
Demo 1
What Model Failure Looks Like (15 min)
Facilitator runs a live demonstration using a simplified claims frequency model. Participants watch as the model is fed post-COVID data and its outputs silently break — while looking perfectly healthy on standard metrics.
Tool used: Python notebook projected live (or pre-run with outputs visible). Demonstrates: (1) data drift detection, (2) Gini coefficient staying stable while predictions deteriorate, (3) a calibration plot that reveals the hidden problem. No coding required from participants.
0:20
Framework
The Governance Landscape in 5 Minutes (5 min)
A single reference slide maps the terrain: APRA CPG 234, Actuaries Institute AI guidance (2023), ASIC expectations on algorithmic decision-making, and where the three lines of defence sit in relation to AI models.
Key point: Governance frameworks don't tell you if a model is good — they tell you who is responsible for checking, and what evidence they must produce. The actuarial and risk functions have different mandates, both are necessary.
0:25
Demo 2
Tiering a Model — Live Walk-Through (10 min)
Facilitator applies a three-tier model risk framework to three real actuarial AI use cases in real time, talking through the reasoning out loud. Participants call out factors they think matter.
Three cases shown:
• A GPT-powered policy wording summariser (Tier 1 — low)
• An ML claims triage model routing claims to fast-track (Tier 2 — medium)
• A neural net reserving model used in the APRA Liability return (Tier 3 — high)

Tiering criteria covered: regulatory materiality, consumer impact, model replaceability, data sensitivity, interpretability.
0:35
Activity 1
Tier It Yourself (15 min)
Mixed groups of 3–4 (at least one actuary + one risk/compliance per group) receive a one-page model description and must agree on a tier, list three validation requirements, and identify who owns each requirement.
Case assigned: A life insurer's ML-driven lapse propensity model used to trigger retention offers — feeding into assumptions used in the EV calculation.

Deliverable: Groups fill in a pre-printed "Model Risk Card" (one per group). No laptops needed — pen and paper.
0:50
Debrief
Tier Reveal + Discussion (10 min)
Groups share their tier and one surprising disagreement from within the group. Facilitator reveals the "reference answer" and unpacks where actuarial and risk perspectives typically diverge — and why both are valid.
Discussion prompt: "Your group probably disagreed about whether the EV link makes this Tier 2 or Tier 3. Let's talk about that — because regulators will ask the same question."
1:00
Activity 2
Write the Finding (20 min)
Same groups receive a pre-filled model validation report with three "issues" buried in technical language. They must: (1) identify the material issue, (2) rewrite it as a governance finding, (3) assign a risk rating and owner.
Format provided: A structured finding template — Issue / Evidence / Risk Rating / Recommended Action / Owner / Due Date.

Constraint: The finding must be readable by both the Appointed Actuary and the CRO without a translation layer. Actuaries in the group must resist over-specifying; risk professionals must resist being vague.
1:20
Close
Findings Swap + Wrap-Up (10 min)
Groups swap their written findings with another group for a 90-second peer review: "Would your CRO understand this? Would your Appointed Actuary trust it?" Then one group reads theirs aloud.
Facilitator close: Three things to take back to your desk today — (1) the tiering criteria, (2) the finding template, (3) the question: who in your organisation owns AI model monitoring after go-live?
Facilitator Demonstrations

Both demonstrations are designed to be run live, with the facilitator narrating their reasoning. Participants observe — they do not need computers for these segments.

DEMO 01 · 15 MIN
When a Healthy Model Goes Wrong
"The Gini looks fine. The model passed validation. Why are we losing money on motor?"
A pre-built Python notebook simulates a motor claims frequency model trained on 2019 data. The facilitator feeds it 2022 data (post-COVID urban driving patterns) and runs three diagnostics live.
  1. Show Gini coefficient — still 0.41, looks fine
  2. Show calibration plot — actual vs predicted diverging badly by distance band
  3. Show Population Stability Index — PSI > 0.25 flagging distribution shift
  4. Ask: "Which of these three is the real governance trigger?"
Key insight: A model can pass its validation metrics and still be materially wrong. Governance requires ongoing monitoring, not just point-in-time sign-off.
DEMO 02 · 10 MIN
Tiering Three Models in Real Time
"Should the same validation standard apply to a chatbot and a reserving model?"
Facilitator projects a blank tiering matrix and populates it live, asking participants to call out factors. Three Australian insurance AI use cases are worked through in sequence.
  1. GPT policy summariser — quick group vote, discuss why it's Tier 1
  2. ML claims triage — introduce consumer impact and replaceability as Tier 2 drivers
  3. Neural net reserving model — APRA materiality, Tier 3 requirements explained
Key insight: Tier determines validation depth, sign-off authority, and monitoring frequency — not just who reviews the model documentation.
Participant Activities

Both activities are designed for mixed groups of 3–4. Each group must include at least one actuary and one risk or compliance professional. Activities are deliberately impossible to complete well with only one discipline.

ACTIVITY 01 · 15 MIN
Tier the Lapse Model
Your life insurer has built an ML model predicting which policyholders are likely to lapse in the next 90 days. Retention teams use its output to trigger proactive calls and offers. The model's predictions also feed into the lapse assumption used in the embedded value (EV) calculation.
Groups receive a one-page model description and a blank Model Risk Card. They must reach consensus on three things:
  1. Assign a tier (1, 2, or 3) with written justification
  2. List three specific validation requirements for that tier
  3. Name the owner of each requirement (actuary / risk / compliance / IT)
Deliberate tension: The EV link arguably makes this Tier 3. The retail contact trigger arguably makes it Tier 2. Groups must argue this out — there is no clean answer.
ACTIVITY 02 · 20 MIN
Write the Governance Finding
You have reviewed a model validation report for a health insurer's ML-based hospital admission predictor used in group pricing. The report is technically thorough but buries three issues in statistical language. One of them is material.
Groups receive a two-page excerpt from a fictional validation report. They must:
  1. Identify which of the three flagged issues is the material governance finding
  2. Rewrite it using the provided Finding Template (Issue / Evidence / Risk Rating / Action / Owner / Due Date)
  3. Assign a risk rating: Low / Medium / High / Critical
  4. Swap with another group — peer review in 90 seconds
The buried issue: The model was validated on claims data from large employers only, but is now being applied to SME groups — a population materially outside the training distribution.
