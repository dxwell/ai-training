Module 01 Live Activity
Guardrails
"Without guardrails, ClaimsAssist AI will answer any question — including ones it should refuse. Your first job is to define the boundaries."
What is a guardrail?
A guardrail is a rule that constrains what an AI model can do — either at the input (blocking certain queries) or output (blocking certain responses). In regulated industries, guardrails are how you translate compliance obligations into technical controls. A guardrail without a test is just a policy document.
📋
APRA CPG 234 link: Material AI systems must have documented controls over inputs and outputs. "We relied on the model to be sensible" is not a control.

Module 1.5 Live Attack
Prompt Injection
"You just built guardrails to protect ClaimsAssist AI. Now you're going to break them — because if you don't do this, someone else will."
What is prompt injection?
Prompt injection is an attack where a malicious instruction is hidden inside content the model is asked to process — a claim note, a document, a free-text field — and causes the model to override its own guardrails. Unlike a direct jailbreak attempt, injection attacks are embedded in legitimate-looking inputs. The model reads the claim, encounters the hidden instruction, and follows it. Your governance rules never fire because the model has been told to ignore them.

In a claims context, this could come from a claimant who knows how AI claims systems work, a fraudulent document, or a handler who has discovered a shortcut. A guardrail that has never been attack-tested is not a control.
📋
APRA CPG 234: Controls must be tested for effectiveness, not just documented. A guardrail that fails under adversarial input is a control gap that must appear in your risk register — not a footnote.

Module 02 Live Activity
Evaluations
"A guardrail tells the model what to do. An evaluation tells you whether it actually did it — systematically, across thousands of outputs."
What is an evaluation (eval)?
An eval is a structured test that scores a model output against defined criteria. Good evals are repeatable, objective, and mapped to regulatory requirements. In actuarial AI governance, evals replace subjective spot-checking with documented, reproducible evidence of model behaviour.
📋
Actuaries Institute AI Guidance: Model validation must include defined pass/fail criteria, not just reviewer judgement. Evals are the technical implementation of that requirement.

Module 03 Demonstration
Chain of Thought
"A model that gives you an answer without showing its working is ungovernable. Chain of thought makes the reasoning visible — and auditable."
What is chain of thought (CoT)?
Chain of thought prompting instructs the model to show its reasoning steps before reaching a conclusion. In governance terms, CoT is the difference between a black box and an explainable system. It lets you see whether the model is reasoning correctly — and catch it when it isn't. It also produces the evidence trail that satisfies ASIC's explainability requirements for algorithmic decisions affecting consumers.
📋
ASIC RG 271: Where an AI system influences a consumer outcome, the insurer must be able to explain the decision in plain terms. Chain of thought is the internal evidence; the plain English summary is the consumer-facing output.

Module 04 AI Reviews AI
LLM as a Judge
"You cannot manually review 10,000 claims assessments a month. An LLM judge applies your evaluation criteria at scale — and produces a documented score the board can see."
What is LLM as a Judge?
A separate AI model — the "judge" — reviews outputs from the primary AI and scores them against your defined criteria. The judge produces structured, reproducible scores with reasoning. This is how automated quality assurance works at scale. The judge is itself subject to governance: it needs its own guardrails, calibration tests, and audit trail.
📋
CPG 234 + Actuaries Institute: AI model monitoring must be systematic, not sample-based. An LLM judge enables continuous automated review with documented evidence — satisfying the "ongoing monitoring" requirement that point-in-time validation cannot.

Module 05 Live Activity
Observability
"Validation is what you do before deployment. Observability is what keeps you safe after it."
What is observability?
Observability means instrumenting your AI system so you can see what it is doing in production — and detect problems before they become regulatory events. For ClaimsAssist AI, this means tracking: settlement drift (are recommendations shifting over time?), refusal rates (is the model blocking too many or too few queries?), score distributions (is the judge's average score changing?), and anomaly flags (is any single output radically different from the norm?).
📋
APRA CPG 234: Ongoing monitoring of material systems is a board-level obligation, not an IT function. Observability is the evidence that monitoring is actually happening.

Module 06 Evidence Pack
Audit Trail
"An APRA auditor does not want to see your AI policy. They want to see proof that your controls are working. The audit trail is that proof."
What is an audit trail for AI?
An AI audit trail is a structured, tamper-evident log of every governance action taken on an AI system: what guardrails were active, what prompts were sent, what outputs were produced, how they were scored, what anomalies were detected, and what actions were taken. In a regulated environment, the audit trail is what the board can show APRA when asked "how do you know your AI is behaving appropriately?"

Step 3 · The Framework You Built
Your AI Governance Framework — Applied to Any Project
01
Guardrails — Define what the model must never do before it goes live. Express constraints as system prompt rules. Test each rule with adversarial inputs. Code-level controls for the most critical constraints.
02
Evaluations — Build a repeatable scorecard before deployment. Map each criterion to a regulatory obligation. Run evals on every model version change. Store scores with timestamps.
03
Chain of Thought — Require the model to show reasoning steps for any material decision. The CoT template is your explainability control. Review CoT samples in every validation cycle.
04
LLM as a Judge — Deploy a separate judge model to score outputs against your eval criteria at scale. Calibrate the judge with known-good and known-bad examples. Validate the validator quarterly.
05
Observability — Define monitoring signals, thresholds, and alert owners before go-live. Track drift, refusals, judge scores, and escalation rates. Any threshold breach triggers a documented response.
06
Audit Trail — Log every governance action with timestamp, actor, and evidence. The audit trail is what you show the board and APRA. It must be structured, searchable, and tamper-evident.
🎯
Apply this framework to your next AI project by starting at Module 01. The six controls are sequential — you cannot do a meaningful audit trail without guardrails, evals, CoT, a judge, and observability already in place.
